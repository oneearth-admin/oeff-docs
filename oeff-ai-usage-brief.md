# OEFF — AI Tools Usage Brief

**Date:** February 2026
**Author:** Garen Glazier, Technical Coordinator
**Status:** Internal working document — not policy, not mandate

---

## What this is

A short guide to how OEFF uses AI tools in our workflow, what we use them for, and what we don't. This isn't a formal policy — it's a transparent account of current practice so team members can make informed choices about whether and how to use these tools.

## What we use

**Claude** (by Anthropic) — a conversational AI assistant. Used for:

- Drafting host communications (emails, reminders, follow-ups)
- Processing and organizing data (spreadsheet cleanup, survey analysis, schedule reconciliation)
- Creating templates and documentation
- Summarizing meeting notes and generating follow-up action items
- Thinking through comms strategy and sequencing

**NotebookLM** (by Google) — a document analysis tool. Useful for:

- Exploring large document sets (past comms, meeting notes, planning docs)
- Asking questions about uploaded documents
- Getting oriented in unfamiliar material

## What we don't use AI for

- **Final outbound communications.** AI drafts. Humans review and send. No exceptions.
- **Decisions about people, budget, or scope.** AI can organize information to support decisions. It doesn't make them.
- **Donor, sponsor, or financial data processing.** Sensitive information stays out of AI tools.
- **Public-facing content without review.** Anything hosts, filmmakers, or audiences see gets human eyes before it goes out.
- **Replacing human judgment on tone.** OEFF's voice is relational — partnership, not institutional. AI gets close but needs human calibration.

## The environmental question

AI tools consume energy. Language models require significant compute. This is real, and worth naming for an environmental film festival.

The honest accounting:

- **What it costs:** Each conversation uses energy equivalent to a few web searches. A heavy month of use is comparable to streaming a few hours of video.
- **What it saves:** Hours of manual data processing, template creation, and communication drafting that would otherwise fall on a very small team. OEFF has 3-5 active team members producing a festival with 16+ screenings across Chicago.
- **The tradeoff we've accepted:** Using AI tools to make a small team's data-heavy processes humane is a reasonable use of compute — especially when the alternative is burnout or dropping work that directly supports the festival's mission.

This isn't a settled question. It's an ongoing one. If you have thoughts about it, bring them — that conversation is welcome.

## Guidelines for team members

1. **You don't have to use AI tools.** They're available, not required.
2. **If you use them, review the output.** AI drafts are starting points, not finished work.
3. **Don't put sensitive data into AI tools.** No donor info, financial data, or personal details beyond what's needed for the task.
4. **Keep the human in the loop for anything outbound.** Draft with AI, send as yourself.
5. **Ask Garen if you're unsure.** No question about tool usage is a dumb question.

## What this document is NOT

- Not a mandate to use AI tools
- Not a justification for replacing any role or function
- Not a formal board-level policy (that may come later, with more data)
- Not permanent — this will evolve as we learn what works
