Calendar invite issue (item 7)

What happened: Wrong host names appeared — multiple people showed as Kim.

Frame to use: Lead with the system fix, not the error.

"A few hosts got calendar invites with the wrong name. I've added a name-field check to the QC step before sends go out — should catch it going forward."

If Ana raises it first:

"Yeah, I caught that. The pre-send check wasn't catching name fields — it's fixed now."
Do NOT say: "Anyone could have made this mistake." (True, but sounds defensive. The system-fix frame is stronger.)
Do NOT over-apologize. One acknowledgment, one fix, move on.

ASAP pattern (item 8)

Your chosen approach: Incident-based — use the calendar invite as the opening.

The bridge sentence:

"The invite issue is a good example of what happens when I can't tell what's today vs this-week. When everything moves at the same speed, I can't sequence well — and that's where errors happen. I have a small proposal."

Then present the three-tier triage table (Blocking / This week / Next cycle).

Calibration by directness:

LevelLanguage
Gentle"I want to make sure the most urgent things get my best attention. Can we try labeling requests for a couple weeks?"
Direct"When everything is marked urgent, nothing is. The invite mistake happened because I was rushing three things at once. Here's a simple triage I'd like to try."
Very direct"I need you to tell me what's actually blocking vs what's this-week. Right now I can't tell, and it's causing errors. Here's what I'd propose."
Recommended: Start with "direct." You have 6 years of trust. You can be plain.

The one-directional urgency pattern:

Things flow ASAP to you but sit unreviewed on Ana's end. Don't lead with this. But if the conversation opens up:

"I'd also like to set mutual turnaround expectations — if I owe you something by Thursday, I'd like to know when I can expect review back. Not to pressure, just so I can plan my week."

Contracts (item 3)

The ask: A specific date for board review and signature.

"All three contracts are done on my end. What's the timeline for board review? I'd like a date so Erin and Kim aren't in limbo."

If Ana says "soon" or "I'll get to it":

"I hear you — is next Friday realistic? I just want to give Erin and Kim a concrete expectation."
Why this matters: 49 days out. People are working without formal terms. This protects everyone — including Ana — if anything goes sideways.

On-site / festival week (item 4)

This is firm and Ana knows. So the conversation is about coverage, not negotiation.

"I'm not on-site during fest week — you know that. Here's what I'm thinking for coverage: interns can handle [X], Erin covers [Y]. The venues with live captioning need additional video support — that's the gap. Can we map who covers what this week?"

If Ana pushes back:

"I understand. Let me draft the support matrix with the gaps clearly marked, and we can figure out together who fills them. I want to make sure the plan is solid before fest week, not improvised during it."

The translator role

Park this for after fest. The triage proposal is the first step toward formalizing it. If the triage works, the next conversation is: "This routing I'm doing — should we name it? It's becoming a real part of how the team works."

That's a scope/contract conversation for after fest, not a pre-fest negotiation.

General meeting posture

  • Lead with proposals, not problems. Every item above has a "here's what I'd do" attached.
  • Protect the close. Last 5 min: read back decisions, confirm action items, assign owners. Don't compress.
  • One acknowledgment per issue, then move forward. Don't loop back.
  • Watch for the shame spiral. If you start feeling like you're defending yourself, pause. You're not on trial. You're a 6-year partner proposing improvements to shared infrastructure.
