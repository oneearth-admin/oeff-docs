What Changed and Why

The March 13 architecture pivot, in plain language

We moved from organizing data around venues to organizing it around events. This sounds small but it changes everything downstream.

Before (v1)

Venue-Centric

24 shared grid views, one per venue. Multi-event venues had comma-separated values. Kim couldn't edit rollup fields. 69 fields in Venues table, 29 of them formula debt.

Now (v2)

Event-Centric

One row per event. Flat text fields Kim can edit directly. A script assembles data from master tables. 28 fields, all readable. Per-event pages replace per-venue views.

The tradeoff we chose: v2 duplicates data (every event row has its own copy of the venue address) in exchange for editability and clarity. The assembly script can re-flatten at any time without overwriting Kim's manual edits in the 7 protected fields.

Decisions We've Made

7 architecture decisions from our March 13 meeting — the load-bearing walls

1
Decision 1

Events as relational key

Events are the most specific data level. Everything links through them — venues, films, contacts, dates.

"The most specific our data will ever get is by event."Kim
2
Decision 2

Unified Directory

All people — hosts, filmmakers, venue contacts, sponsors — in one table with a Contact Type field. Replaces scattered contact lists.

"What if we just called this table a directory?"Kim
3
Decision 3

Collaborators vs. Transactional

Mission-oriented partners vs. deliverable-based contacts. This distinction shapes how we communicate with everyone in the Directory.

4
Decision 4

Members vs. Partners

14k email list = members (who we work for). Active stakeholders = partners (who we work with). Clarifies the intent of every communication.

"Distinguish who we work with and who we work for."Kim
5
Decision 5

Per-event interface pages

Replace 24 shared grid views with per-event Interface Designer pages. Deep links give direct edit access to a single event without scrolling through 200 rows.

6
Decision 6

Dual venue classification

Flagship/Community (team presence level) alongside T1–T4 (technical needs). Two separate classification systems that don't compete.

7
Decision 7

"Events" umbrella term

"Screenings" becomes a subtype of "events." Future-proofs the model for action fairs, concerts, panels. Full terminology conversation deferred to post-festival.

These are the load-bearing walls. Change one, and the sync scripts, merge sheets, and host pages all need updating. Every downstream tool we build assumes these 7 decisions hold.

Why This Architecture Matters for Sustainability

Research-backed reasoning for the choices we're making

The real risk is continuity, not scale

OEFF won't outgrow Airtable anytime soon. What threatens the system is when someone leaves and their knowledge goes with them. The architecture should be self-documenting enough that a new contractor can operate it within a day.

If Garen leaves

At risk: script refresh, sync triage, Cloudflare deployment. Mitigated by: decision log, MANIFEST.md, the architecture map, this document.

If Kim leaves

At risk: daily data hygiene, comms-state updates, host relationship history. Mitigated by: per-event pages with clear fields, runbooks, field descriptions.

Anti-patterns we're actively avoiding

Anti-patternWhy it matters
Routing via memory only"Ask Garen" is the current routing table. If Garen's unavailable, nobody knows where data lives.

Copy-and-edit workflowsData drift between copies. Our fix: one assembly script, one source of truth per field.
Permission shortcuts for internsBroad editor access. Our fix: interface-only access, narrow forms.
