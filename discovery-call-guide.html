One Earth Film Festival 2026

Discovery Call Guide

Collaborator conversation — not an interview, not a pitch
Before you start

The framing that matters

This person is looking for AV and events experience — that's what they told you, and that's real. OEFF has genuine AV/Ops work to offer. Start there.

They also happen to carry change management and strategic consulting skills from Deloitte. You don't need to name that or steer toward it. If the conversation goes well, that value will surface on its own — and you can open the door gently.

Your job on this call: understand what they want, share what OEFF actually is, and see if there's a natural overlap. Nothing needs to be decided today.

01

Set the table

2–3 minutes
"Thanks for making time for this. I want to be upfront — I don't have a formal job description to hand you. What I have is a real organization doing real work, and I'm trying to figure out where there's a natural fit. So this is genuinely a discovery conversation for both of us."
Why this matters: You're naming the ambiguity early so neither of you has to pretend there's a polished role waiting. This gives you both permission to explore honestly.
02

What OEFF actually is

3–5 minutes

Tell them in your own words, but hit these points:

  • The model: Distributed film festival across ~30 community venues — libraries, churches, cultural centers, plus professional cinemas like Music Box and Gene Siskel. Not one big event, a network of screenings. This is the thing that surprises people and hooks the right ones.
  • The range: Some venues are turnkey (projector, sound, staff). Some need full AV support. A venue tier system governs which get what level of production. If they light up at the logistics complexity, that's a signal.
  • The team: Small. Ana is the Executive Director and only full-time staff. Everyone else is part-time, contractor, or volunteer. You've been Technical Coordinator for six years.
  • The season: 12th year. Festival is in April. Planning is happening now.
03

Learn what they're looking for

8–10 minutes
  • "What's drawing you to events and AV work specifically?" Listen for: curiosity vs. credential-seeking. Both are fine, but they lead to different placements.
  • "When you picture yourself doing this kind of work, what does a good day look like?" Listen for: do they describe being on-site at events, or do they describe behind-the-scenes coordination? Both are needed.
  • "You mentioned being open to any positions. If you could design your own learning path, what would you want to walk away knowing how to do?" Let them paint the picture. Don't correct or redirect yet.
  • "How do you feel about ambiguity? A lot of what we do is figure-it-out-as-we-go, especially with a small team." Deloitte people are trained in structured ambiguity. See if they frame this as comfortable or as something they'd want to bring structure to — both answers are useful.
  • "Walk me through a typical week right now — when do you have bandwidth, and how do you prefer to work?" Confirm the April weekends and weekday evening availability from your earlier exchange.
04

Share the real problems

5–7 minutes

Frame these as the landscape, not as job requirements. You're showing them where the work lives.

"Let me tell you where we actually need help, and you can tell me what resonates."
  • April coverage: "I have a full-time job that makes me largely unavailable during festival month. The festival is in April. So we need people who can hold things during that stretch."
  • Venue coordination: "30 venues, each with different technical capabilities and needs. The coordination between hosts, films, and logistics is where things fall through cracks."
  • Institutional knowledge: "A lot of how this festival runs lives in people's heads — mostly mine and Ana's. We've never been great at documenting our own processes."
  • Onboarding: "Every time we bring someone new on, we're kind of rebuilding the wheel. We don't have a repeatable system for getting people up to speed."
Note: The last two points — process documentation and onboarding — are where their change management skills naturally apply. You're not asking them to consult. You're describing real problems. If they hear those and start thinking structurally, that tells you something.
05

The organizational context

3–5 minutes — only if the conversation is going well

If they seem engaged and you trust the vibe, share the dynamics that would help a change-minded person understand the terrain:

"I want to be honest about where we are organizationally, because it's relevant to anyone coming in."
  • "We've tried system rollouts before — Slack, Google Studio — that were well-intentioned but didn't stick because they lacked upfront strategy. I'm trying not to repeat that pattern."
  • "Role ownership is still fuzzy in some areas. People do good work, but who owns what and where handoffs happen isn't always clear."
  • "The org is founder-driven with a volunteer board. Decisions move through relationships and trust more than formal process."
  • "We're mid-transition from me doing most of the technical work solo to building systems other people can run. That shift is ongoing and imperfect."
What you're doing here: Giving them real organizational signal without assigning blame or asking them to fix it. A change management person hearing this will understand the landscape immediately. Watch whether they ask follow-up questions about these dynamics — that's the strongest signal that this dimension of their skills could be activated.
06

The gentle door-open

2–3 minutes

This is where you acknowledge their broader skill set without reframing the whole conversation around it.

The bridge line

"I know you're looking to build experience in events and AV, and there's real work here for that. But I also want to name — your background in change management is something we don't have anywhere else on this team. If there are moments where that thinking feels useful to you, I'd want to make space for it. Not as a separate consulting engagement, just as part of how you show up."

Why this works: It honors what they asked for (events experience), validates what they bring (strategic skills), and doesn't create pressure to perform in a lane they didn't sign up for. It also signals that you see them as more than a task-doer — which matters for retention.
07

The honest conversation about compensation

2–3 minutes
"I want to be transparent about the budget reality. This is a small nonprofit. We have some contractor budget but it's modest — not consulting rates. Some of the early work might be volunteer-level while we figure out scope together. I don't want to waste your time if that doesn't work for where you are right now."
Let them respond. They told you they're trying to build experience. Many career-changers accept below-market rates for the right learning opportunity. But let them name their own comfort level — don't assume.
08

Close and next steps

2–3 minutes
"Here's what I'd like to do: take what we talked about, think on it, talk to Ana about where this fits, and come back to you with something more concrete — even if it's a small starting project to see how we work together. Does that timeline work for you?"
