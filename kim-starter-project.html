Why this project

OEFF has been building documentation, communication templates, and host-facing resources at speed — but almost entirely from the inside. Everything was written by people who already know the org. We've never had someone with fresh eyes and a process background look at what we've built and tell us what's clear, what's confusing, and what's missing.

This project gives Kim a defined first contribution with a real deliverable, while also getting her oriented to OEFF's model, team, and workflows. She learns the org by reading the org.

Context on where we are: OEFF is in year one of moving from manual coordination to platform-based infrastructure — shared inboxes, a centralized host guide, webinar registration through Zoom. The tools are working, but the team is still building muscle memory. You'll see moments where things are stumbly — a process that's documented but not yet habitual, or a communication that went out before the fix was confirmed. That's adoption, not dysfunction. Part of what makes your fresh-eyes perspective valuable is seeing these patterns from the outside.

What she'll review

Primary materials

  • Host Guide (hosts.oneearthfilmfest.org) — the main resource hosts receive for screening logistics, setup, and support
  • Team Documentation site (docs.oneearthfilmfest.org) — internal team reference, processes, and onboarding info
  • Host communication templates — email scripts and outreach language currently in use or in draft

Secondary context (provided by Garen)

  • Venue tier system overview — how OEFF classifies and supports different venue types
  • Brief team roster — who does what, in broad strokes
  • The shared mailbox migration plan — where email communication is heading

The ask

Review each resource as if you're encountering OEFF for the first time. Write up your observations in whatever format feels natural — a doc, a set of notes, an annotated list. The goal is honest signal, not a polished report.

Three questions to anchor the audit

1. What's unclear? Where would a new host, volunteer, or team member get stuck? What assumes context they don't have?

2. What's missing? Are there obvious gaps — steps that aren't documented, decisions that aren't explained, resources that are referenced but don't exist yet?

3. What could be systematized? Where do you see repeated patterns that could become templates, checklists, or standard sequences? This is where your process background is the most valuable.

How it works

  1. Kickoff (30 min sync with Garen) Walk through the materials, answer initial questions, share context on where things stand. Garen provides access and any login credentials needed.
  2. Independent review (6-8 hours over ~10 days) Kim works through the materials at her own pace. Deep focus work — evenings and weekends as described on the call. Questions can go to Garen via text/email async.
  3. Deliverable: Audit summary A written summary organized around the three anchor questions. Doesn't need to be long or polished. Honest observations are the deliverable.
  4. Debrief (30 min sync with Garen) Walk through findings together. Identify which observations are quick fixes, which are systemic, and which point to the next piece of work.

What success looks like

For Kim

  • Understands OEFF's model, team, and current state
  • Has a concrete deliverable she can point to
  • Gets a feel for the working relationship before committing to more

For OEFF

  • Gets a process-trained outside perspective on its documentation
  • Identifies gaps before hosts encounter them during festival prep
  • Evaluates fit and working style with low stakes

For Garen

  • Sees how Kim thinks — does she flag surface issues or structural ones?
  • Gets signal on whether the change management skills are active and applicable
  • Low onboarding cost — the review is the onboarding

Compensation

This is a paid project. Rate and total to be confirmed with Kim before kickoff, drawn from the collaborator pool within the OEFF technical budget.

Keeping the first project paid — even at a modest rate — sets the right precedent. It signals that OEFF values people's time and avoids the dynamic where volunteer work quietly becomes the expectation.

What could come after this

If the audit goes well and both sides want to continue, natural next projects include:

  • Host communication sequence design — building the outreach cadence and templates for the shared mailbox
  • Onboarding pathway documentation — creating a repeatable process so the next new person doesn't start from scratch
  • Festival week venue coordination — on-the-ground support during April 22-28, leveraging her weekend availability
  • Post-festival retrospective facilitation — using her change management lens to run a structured debrief

None of these are committed. They're visible possibilities that give shape to what a longer collaboration could look like.
