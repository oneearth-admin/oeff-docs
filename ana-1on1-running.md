## Communication frameworks

Tested structures for this specific relationship. Review before each call — pick 1-2 to practice, not all of them.

### Structure: What → So What → Now What (Abrahams)

Every agenda item gets three beats:
1. **What** — the situation, plain facts. "Seven films are ready. Two are on hold."
2. **So What** — why it matters to her, to the team, to the timeline. "If we send today, filmmakers have 6 weeks to respond."
3. **Now What** — the specific ask. "I need a green light on the templates."

This is BLUF for verbal processors. Ana needs the narrative arc, not just the bottom line — but the arc needs to land somewhere specific.

### Move: Paraphrase before pivoting

Before steering away from a tangent or toward a decision, reflect back what she said. Not word-for-word — the *meaning*.

> Ana: "I'm just thinking about whether the Dominican venue is really the right fit for this film, because last year we had that issue with..."
> You: "So you're weighing whether Dominican can handle the screening format. Let me show you what I've got on venue capacity — that might answer it."

Why this works: She feels heard. You get to steer. Nobody gets interrupted.

### Move: Silence after the ask

When you ask for a decision or a date, stop talking. Don't fill. Don't offer an easier version. Don't say "or we could..." Count to five internally.

Ana processes out loud. The silence is where she starts thinking. If you fill it, she'll respond to your filler instead of the question.

**Practice targets:** Contract signature date. Which 2 screenings are captioned. Go/no-go on templates.

### Move: Bookend close

Mirror the opening energy at the end. If you opened collaborative ("we've both been in this doc"), close collaborative ("here's what we landed on together"). If you opened with urgency ("48 days"), close with progress ("two big things off the board today").

The close is what she carries into the next conversation. Make it feel like movement, not a task list.

### Move: "What would help most?"

When she signals overwhelm ("there's so much to do"), don't offer solutions. Ask: "What would take the most off your plate right now?" This redirects from anxiety to agency. Let her name the thing — then you can offer to take it.

### Anti-moves (things that don't work with Ana)

- **Presenting a finished system for approval.** She needs to feel like a collaborator, not a rubber stamp. Even if you built it yourself, frame it as "here's a draft — what do you think?"
- **Technical walkthroughs.** She doesn't need to know how the merge sheet works. She needs to know it's handled.
- **Stacking structural asks.** One proposal per meeting. Triage OR turnaround expectations OR naming — never all three.
- **Writing when talking would work.** Some things need the relational channel. If you've sent two emails and gotten no response, it's a call item.

## Reflection protocol

Use after every call. Takes 5 minutes. The post-meeting section in each meeting history entry should capture these, not just freeform notes.

### Prompts (answer 2-3, not all)

1. **What decision actually got made?** (Not "what did we discuss" — what's different now?)
2. **What did I say that landed?** (The sentence that got a "yes" or shifted the energy.)
3. **What did I say that didn't land?** (The moment she went quiet, changed subject, or said "let me think about it.")
4. **Did I fill a silence I should have held?**
5. **Did I stack when I should have stopped?** (Won the first thing, then kept going.)
6. **What did she raise that I didn't expect?** (This is where her real priorities live.)
7. **What's the one thing I'd do differently next time?**

### Pattern tracker

After 3-4 meetings, look for:
- Which framework am I actually using vs. which ones am I forgetting?
- Which of "my patterns" am I catching in real-time vs. only in reflection?
- Is the meeting facilitation protocol (D/Disc/I, readback close) holding, or has it drifted?
- Are parked items getting raised at the right time, or staying parked out of avoidance?
